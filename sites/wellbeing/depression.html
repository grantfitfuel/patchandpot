Understanding Depression

Depression is more than sadness. It can feel like life has lost colour: low energy, foggy thinking, heaviness, guilt, and disconnection. You’re not weak or broken — you’re having a human response to difficult biology and difficult life stuff. This page offers clear explanations, everyday examples, and practical steps you can try, at your pace.

1) What depression can feel like

  • Body: heavy limbs, fatigue, sleep changes, appetite shifts, slower movement.
  • Mind: fog, indecision, loss of motivation or pleasure, harsh self-talk, guilt/hopelessness.
  • Behaviour: withdrawing, postponing tasks, losing routines, “I’ll do it later” loops.
  • Waves: some hours feel lighter, others heavier — not linear, not your fault.

If you notice thoughts of self-harm or not wanting to be alive, skip down to Safety plan and Support.

2) Symptoms & common types

Core symptoms (NICE-style)

  • Persistent low mood and/or loss of interest/pleasure
  • Fatigue, sleep/appetite changes, concentration problems
  • Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt
  • Recurrent thoughts of death/self-harm

Patterns

  • Mild: functioning with difficulty; still helpful to treat
  • Moderate: clear impact on work/relationships
  • Severe: marked impairment; may include psychotic features

Variants

  • SAD: seasonal pattern (shorter days)
  • Perinatal: during pregnancy/after birth
  • Masked in men: irritability, risk-taking, overwork

3) Why it happens (biopsychosocial — not your fault)

  • Biology: sleep debt, pain, hormones, nutrition deficits, medications.
  • Psychology: perfectionism, rumination, harsh rules (“must”, “should”).
  • Social: loneliness, money/work stress, grief, caring responsibilities, discrimination.

Depression is multi-factor. That means many small levers to pull — good news for recovery.

4) When to seek help

  • Symptoms most days for >2 weeks
  • Work/study/relationships affected
  • Self-harm thoughts or plans (seek urgent help)

Start with: your GP, local NHS talking therapies, or helplines below. Tell one safe person today.

5) Safety plan (for tough moments)

  1. Warning signs: “When I stop replying / stay in bed.”
  2. Reasons to stay: names, places, hopes, pets, responsibilities.
  3. Distraction list (10–60 min): shower, walk, favourite series, call, puzzle.
  4. People to contact: friend, family, neighbour, peer group, GP.
  5. Professional supports: Samaritans 116 123 (24/7), NHS urgent lines.
  6. Environment: reduce access to means; store meds safely; remove triggers where possible.

Print a one-page plan and keep it visible. See Printables.

6) Foundations that make everything else easier

Sleep (aim for “good enough”)

  • Fixed wake time; light to eyes within 30 minutes if possible
  • Wind-down: dim lights, phone out of reach, repeatable routine
  • If awake >20 mins: get up briefly; low-stimulation activity

Movement (minimums count)

  • 2–10 minutes daily “movement snacks” (walk, stairs, stretch)
  • Sunlight + fresh air when possible; music helps

Food & hydration

  • Simple structure: 3 “good-enough” meals or 4–5 snacks
  • Protein + fibre baseline; keep fruit/veg visible
  • Glass of water per meal; mug by the kettle as cue

7) Behavioural activation (BA): do tiny, meaningful things

Depression says “wait until you feel like it.” BA says “do a tiny thing first; feelings follow behaviour.”

Values mini-map

Pick 3 areas: body, connection, home, learning, outdoors, creativity, money, care.

Write one 2-minute action for each.

Starter steps (2–5 mins)

  • Open the window and breathe 6 slow exhales
  • Wash face & teeth; fresh T-shirt
  • Text “No need to reply — saying hi” to a safe person
  • Put one plate in the sink; start the tap

Weekly template

  • Mon/Wed/Fri: 5-min walk
  • Tue/Thu: 5-min tidy + bin
  • Weekend: one joy seed (music, plant, recipe)

8) Thoughts & rumination (get unstuck, gently)

Unhook from loops

  • Label: “I’m having the thought that I’m a failure.”
  • Timer: 5-minute “worry window” → then single action
  • Write it, fold it, move body 60 seconds

Reframes (accurate, not fake)

  • “I never finish” → “I finish small things when I start small.”
  • “Pointless” → “Unclear now; I’ll test one tiny step.”
  • “Everyone copes but me” → “People show highlights; I’m human.”

Self-talk ladders

  • “I can’t” → “I can try 60 seconds.”
  • “I’m broken” → “I’m having a hard season.”

See Neuroscience → Reframing and Neurolinguistics for scripts & cards.

9) Connection & work/study

Connection (low-energy)

  • “Two-line text”: “Energy low. Thinking of you.”
  • Body-doubling: video on mute for 20 minutes while you do one task
  • Community: library groups, gentle classes, gardening clubs

Work/study adjustments

  • Clear written tasks + examples; shorter meetings
  • Quiet space / camera-off option
  • Flexible deadlines during flare-ups

Speak to HR/student support; you may be protected by equality legislation in the UK.

10) Therapies & medicines (overview)

Talking therapies

  • CBT / Behavioural Activation: thoughts & actions
  • ACT: unhook from thoughts; values-based action
  • MBCT: mindfulness to reduce relapse
  • IPT: relationships & life events

Medicines

  • Antidepressants can help moderate–severe depression
  • Benefits usually build over weeks; review side-effects with your prescriber
  • Often combined with therapy & lifestyle supports

Information only — speak to your GP/pharmacist for medical advice.

11) Special contexts

Perinatal

Depression during/after pregnancy is common and treatable. Ask your midwife/GP; perinatal teams exist to help.

SAD

Light therapy boxes (10,000 lux), morning daylight, routine, Vitamin D (check GP advice) can help seasonal dips.

Neurodiversity

Autism/ADHD often overlap with depression. Lean on sensory comfort, body-doubling, visual timers, 2-minute starts.

Grief vs. depression

Grief has waves tied to loss; depression feels globally empty. They can co-exist — support helps either way.

Men

May show as irritability, overwork, risk-taking, substance use. Different look, same need for support.

Teens

More sleep, irritability, school changes. Ask school to help; CAMHS/young-people’s services exist.

12) Where to find support (UK & Scotland)

  • Samaritans — 24/7 listening (UK & ROI): 116 123
  • Breathing Space (Scotland): evenings & weekends
  • Mind — information & local services (England & Wales)
  • NHS Inform (Scotland) — mental health services & self-help
  • NHS urgent mental health helplines (England, 24/7)
  • Shout — UK crisis textline (text SHOUT to 85258)
  • Childline — for children/young people: 0800 1111

Important Note

The information on this page is for general understanding and support. It is not a substitute for professional medical, psychological, or crisis services. If you feel unable to keep yourself or someone else safe, call 999 (UK) immediately. If you’re outside the UK, contact your local emergency number.

For non-emergency concerns, speak with your GP or a qualified mental health professional, and consider the services listed above.
