Crisis Support

If you are feeling overwhelmed, unsafe, or unable to cope, you are not alone. This page explains what is happening in your mind and body during crisis, offers practical grounding tools, and connects you to immediate support services in the UK.

1. Why crisis feels overwhelming

When anxiety, depression, or trauma overwhelm the mind, the body’s emergency system takes over:

  • Fight or flight: Racing thoughts, pounding heart, tunnel vision.
  • Freeze or shutdown: Numbness, emptiness, unable to speak or move.
  • Overwhelm: A flood of emotion, where logic and calm feel unreachable.

This is your nervous system’s way of protecting you. It is not weakness — it is survival mode.

2. Grounding tools in the moment

  • 5–4–3–2–1: Name 5 things you see, 4 you feel, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, 1 you taste.
  • Box breathing: Inhale for 4, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4. Repeat three times.
  • Temperature shift: Hold something cold, splash water on your face — helps reset panic.
  • Safe touch: Place your hand over your chest, breathe slowly, and say: “I am safe right now.”

These techniques do not “fix everything” but they buy you a pause, giving you space to reach for help.

3. If you are in immediate danger

If you feel you may act on your thoughts or have already harmed yourself:

  • Call 999 in the UK for emergency help.
  • Go to your nearest Accident & Emergency department.

4. If you are not in immediate danger

There are people ready to listen, day or night:

  • Samaritans: Call 116 123 (free, 24/7) or visit online.
  • Shout: Text SHOUT to 85258 (24/7 crisis text support).
  • Breathing Space (Scotland): 0800 83 85 87 (Mon–Thu 6pm–2am, Fri 6pm–Mon 6am).
  • SAMH (Scotland): Scottish Association for Mental Health.
  • Mind (England & Wales): 0300 123 3393 or mind.org.uk.
  • CALL Helpline (Wales): 0800 132 737 (24/7) or text HELP to 81066.
  • Lifeline (Northern Ireland): 0808 808 8000 (24/7 support).

5. After the crisis passes

Recovery starts small. Helpful steps include:

  • Speaking with a trusted friend or family member.
  • Writing down one thought or feeling to reduce the mental load.
  • Seeking longer-term support — such as counselling, peer groups, or your GP if you feel able.
  • Adding a grounding habit to your routine (for example: mindful breathing while watering plants).

Disclaimer

This page is intended as a general wellbeing and support resource. It is not a substitute for professional medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. Always seek advice from a qualified health professional if you have concerns about your mental health.

If you are in crisis or feel unable to keep yourself safe, call 999 in the UK or go to your nearest Accident & Emergency department. For non-emergency urgent support, call NHS 111 (England, Scotland, Wales).
