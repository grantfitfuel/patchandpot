Understanding Depression

Depression isn’t just “feeling sad.” It can flatten energy, numb emotions, and distort thinking. It’s one of the most common mental health experiences worldwide, and in the UK millions live with it every year. Understanding the biology, the daily impact, and the supports available can make it less overwhelming — and remind you that you’re not alone.

1. Understanding Depression

Depression can look like sadness, but also like flatness, irritability, or exhaustion. It affects body, mind, and behaviour:

  • Low energy or constant fatigue, even with rest
  • Loss of interest or pleasure in things once enjoyed
  • Sleep changes: difficulty falling asleep, waking early, or oversleeping
  • Appetite changes: eating much less or more
  • Negative self-talk: “I’m useless,” “Nothing will help”
  • Physical symptoms: aches, slower movements, gut issues

2. Biology / How It Happens

Depression is not a sign of weakness. It reflects changes across several systems in the brain and body. Understanding these helps explain why depression feels heavy — and why multiple approaches often work best.

Neurotransmitters brain chemicals

  • Serotonin — mood stability, sleep, appetite. Disrupted signalling links to low mood and irregular sleep.
  • Dopamine — motivation, reward, drive. Lower dopamine feels like “no spark” (anhedonia).
  • Norepinephrine — energy, alertness, focus. Low levels → fatigue and reduced concentration.
  • GABA — calming, reduces over-activation. Low GABA → restlessness, irritability, poor sleep quality.
  • Cortisol — stress hormone. Can be chronically high/flattened in depression, disturbing sleep, memory, and immunity.
Stabilisers: regular sleep/wake times; daylight exposure (morning light); gentle daily activity; balanced meals with protein and omega-3s; therapies (e.g., CBT/ACT). Medications (e.g., SSRIs/SNRIs) can support signalling where appropriate via your GP.

Hormones body regulators

  • Oestrogen & Progesterone — fluctuate across the menstrual cycle, perimenopause, and menopause. Changes can affect serotonin/dopamine and GABA, increasing vulnerability to low mood, anxiety, and sleep disruption.
  • Testosterone (men & women) — low levels may reduce energy, motivation, and libido; mood can feel flat.
  • Thyroid hormones (T3/T4/TSH) — hypothyroidism can mimic or worsen depression (fatigue, low mood, brain fog).
  • Insulin & blood sugar — large swings can trigger irritability, lethargy, and “brain crashes.”
Stabilisers:
  • Perimenopause/menopause: discuss HRT options with your clinician; CBT for menopause; strength training; consistent sleep routine.
  • Thyroid: GP blood tests; treating underactive thyroid often improves mood/energy.
  • Testosterone: GP assessment if persistent low energy/libido; treat if deficient.
  • Blood sugar: include protein & fibre at meals; steady meal times; limit ultra-processed sugars.

Treatment often combines talking therapies (CBT, ACT), medication (SSRIs, SNRIs, others), and lifestyle supports (movement, sleep, nutrition). Your GP can coordinate investigations (e.g., thyroid, iron, B12, hormones) and referrals to talking therapy hubs.

3. Day-to-day coping

  • Keep a simple routine: meals, getting dressed, light activity
  • Break tasks into tiny steps: “Open laptop” can be a full task
  • Allow rest without guilt — energy is genuinely reduced
  • Reach out: text a friend, call a helpline, or use a journal
Try Behavioural Activation: pick one Body action (2–10 min), one Connection action, and one Meaning/Pleasure action for today. See our planner (PDF).

5. Hope & Recovery

With the right mix of therapy, support, and sometimes medication, many people recover from depression or learn to manage it so life becomes meaningful again. Small consistent steps matter more than dramatic leaps.

6. Self-talk Upgrades

From: “I’m useless.”
To: “I’m struggling, but still here.”
From: “Nothing will ever help.”
To: “This feels endless, but feelings shift.”
From: “I can’t do anything.”
To: “I can do one tiny step.”

Important Note

The information on this page is for general understanding and support. It is not a substitute for professional medical, psychological, or legal advice. If you feel unable to keep yourself safe or someone else is at risk, call 999 (UK) immediately. If you’re outside the UK, contact your local emergency number.

For non-emergency concerns, consider speaking with a qualified health professional or one of the support services listed above.
