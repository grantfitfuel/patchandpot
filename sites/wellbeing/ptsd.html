PTSD & Trauma Recovery

Post-traumatic stress is a normal response to events that overwhelmed your ability to cope: accidents, assaults, disasters, war, medical emergencies, bereavement, or repeated exposure through work. PTSD happens when the brain stays on high alert and trauma memories don’t file away properly. Recovery is possible — often step by step.

1) How PTSD Shows Up

PTSD symptoms cluster into four groups. You may notice some more than others, and they can fluctuate.

Intrusions

  • Flashbacks (feeling as if it’s happening again), intrusive images, sounds, smells
  • Nightmares or night sweats
  • Sudden body memories (e.g., heart racing, muscle freeze)

Avoidance

  • Avoiding reminders: places, people, news, anniversaries
  • Numbing out with alcohol, work, screens, or isolation

Mood & Cognition

  • Guilt, shame, anger, grief; loss of interest, detachment
  • Negative beliefs: “I’m not safe”, “It was my fault”, “People can’t be trusted”
  • Memory gaps or confusion around the event(s)

Arousal & Reactivity

  • Hypervigilance, exaggerated startle, irritability
  • Sleep problems, concentration issues, risky or self-destructive behaviours
  • Physical symptoms: headaches, gut issues, chronic pain flares

2) What’s Happening in the Brain

Trauma changes the balance between your brain’s alarm and its braking systems.

  • Amygdala (alarm): becomes hypersensitive and learns threat cues too broadly.
  • Hippocampus (context & time-stamping): struggles to file the memory as “past” so fragments feel present.
  • Prefrontal cortex (planning / brakes): goes offline under stress; hard to reason, choose or calm.
  • Chemistry: high noradrenaline/adrenaline; cortisol rhythms disrupted; serotonin/dopamine balance altered.
  • Body systems: autonomic nervous system swings between fight/flight and shutdown (freeze/collapse).

Therapies aim to (1) re-link the memory to time and context, (2) calm the alarm, and (3) build skills to steer state.

3) Complex PTSD & Moral Injury

Complex PTSD (C-PTSD) can develop after long-term, repeated trauma (e.g., abuse, coercive control, captivity). In addition to PTSD symptoms, people may notice:

  • Emotional regulation difficulties (sudden floods or numbness)
  • Negative self-concept (“I am broken/bad”)
  • Relationship difficulties (trust, closeness, boundaries)

Moral injury happens when events deeply violate your values — for example, unavoidable actions in war, witnessing preventable harm, or institutional betrayal. It often brings guilt, shame, anger or grief. Compassion-focused therapy and meaning-making work can help.

4) Immediate Grounding Tools (when the wave hits)

Orient to now

  • Look around and name: 5 things you see, 4 touch, 3 hear, 2 smell, 1 taste.
  • Say the date, your location, and something safe in the room.

Breath & body

  • 4–6 breathing: inhale 4, exhale 6 for 2 minutes.
  • Box breathing: 4 in – 4 hold – 4 out – 4 hold (x4).
  • Anchors: feet to ground, back into chair, press palms together.

Temperature & movement

  • Cool water on wrists/face; hold an ice cube wrapped in cloth.
  • “Shake out” arms/legs; short brisk walk to burn adrenaline.

Words that help

  • “This is a memory, not the event.”
  • “I can ride this wave and let it pass.”
  • “Slow and small is still forward.”

5) Flashbacks & Nightmares

When a flashback starts

  • Say (out loud if you can): “I’m having a flashback. I am in [place], the date is [today].”
  • Ground with senses (cool water / texture / scent).
  • Turn on a light, open a window, orient to exits and people you trust.
  • Use a flashback card in your wallet with 3 steps and an emergency contact.

Nightmares

  • Imagery Rehearsal Therapy (IRT): rewrite the nightmare with a safer ending while awake; rehearse 5–10 minutes daily.
  • Keep a warm re-entry ritual at night: dim lights, steady breath, calming scent or sound, a simple phrase (“Safe enough. Bedtime now”).
  • Ask your GP about treatments; some people benefit from medication such as prazosin for trauma nightmares.

6) Sleep Disturbances

PTSD often disrupts sleep via hyperarousal and nightmares. Helpful approaches:

  • CBT-I basics: regular rise time, wind-down hour, bedroom dark & cool, bed only for sleep & sex, get up if awake > 20 min (see our Sleep & Insomnia page).
  • Pre-sleep decompression: brief journaling/worry list, 4–6 breathing, light stretch, gentle audio (no cliff-hanger shows).
  • Nightmare work: combine IRT with trauma therapy (EMDR/TF-CBT).

7) Trigger / Exposure Plan (go at your own pace)

  1. List triggers (sensory, places, dates, news, social media).
  2. Build a ladder from easiest → hardest. Pair each rung with two grounding skills.
  3. Practise the easy rung until distress reduces; step up slowly. Pause or step back after tough days.
  4. Work with a trauma-trained therapist for stuck points.

Exposure isn’t re-traumatisation — it’s titrated, supported learning that “now is different from then.”

8) Day-to-Day Care (tiny, reliable actions)

Body basics

  • Regular meals & hydration (protein with breakfast steadies cortisol)
  • Movement most days: walk, yoga, light strength — short is fine
  • Reduce alcohol; watch caffeine after midday

Connection & meaning

  • One honest check-in per day (message or call)
  • Small acts of purpose: care for a plant, share a meal, help a neighbour

Therapy & medication

  • Evidence-based: TF-CBT, EMDR, NET (Narrative Exposure Therapy)
  • Talk to your GP about SSRIs/SNRIs or other options where appropriate

Safety plan

  • Early-warning signs, 3 grounding steps, who to contact, crisis numbers
  • Store as a phone note + wallet card

9) For Partners, Friends & Carers

  • Believe their experience; avoid pressuring for details.
  • Agree a signal for “I’m getting flooded” and a go-to grounding step.
  • Use plain, present-tense language during flashbacks: “You’re here with me. It’s Monday. We’re in the kitchen.”
  • Offer specific help (lifts, meals, admin), not vague “let me know”.
  • Care for yourself too: boundaries, respite, debriefs, peer support.

10) Veterans & First Responders (UK)

Repeated exposure, shift work and organisational pressure add unique stress. Confidential, specialist help exists:

Veterans & families

  • Op COURAGE (NHS England) — specialist veterans’ mental health service.
  • Veterans First Point (Scotland) — drop-in, peer support & therapy.
  • Veterans NHS Wales — therapy across Wales.
  • Combat Stress — 24/7 helpline & treatment.
  • Royal British Legion & SSAFA — welfare & family support.

Police, Fire & Ambulance

  • Mind Blue Light — tools & support for emergency services.
  • The Ambulance Staff Charity (TASC)
  • The Fire Fighters Charity
  • Police Care UK

24/7 support

  • Samaritans 116 123 — free, any time.
  • Shout 85258 — UK text line.
  • NHS Inform Scotland | NHS (England) | NHS 111 Wales | NI services

For work-related trauma, speak with your occupational health, federation/union or charity above for confidential advice.

11) Where to Get Help

  • Samaritans — 116 123 (free, 24/7). If you’re in immediate danger, call 999.
  • Shout — text SHOUT to 85258 (24/7).
  • Mind — information & local support.
  • NHS Inform (Scotland) — services near you.
  • NHS urgent mental health helplines (England)
  • Combat Stress (veterans) — 0800 138 1619.

Important Note

The information on this page is for general understanding and support. It is not a substitute for professional medical, psychological, or legal advice. If you feel unable to keep yourself or someone else safe, call 999 (UK) immediately. If you’re outside the UK, contact your local emergency number.

For non-emergency concerns, consider speaking with a GP, a trauma-trained therapist, or one of the services listed above.
