Grief: Understanding, Living With, and Finding Support

Grief is not a single feeling but a whole landscape of emotions, thoughts, and physical sensations. It can feel messy, unpredictable, and deeply exhausting — and yet, grief is a sign of love. This page offers explanations, everyday stories, and gentle tools to help you feel less alone.

What Grief Can Feel Like

Grief doesn’t follow neat stages. One day you may feel a wave of sadness, the next a sense of numbness, and later still a burst of laughter that feels almost wrong. All of this is normal.

Everyday story: After losing her partner, Sarah found herself standing in the supermarket frozen in front of the bread aisle. She wasn’t just choosing food — she was facing the empty space at the table. That everyday moment felt heavier than the funeral.

Affirmation: “However it feels today, it is allowed.”

Different Types of Grief

Bereavement

The most recognised form, grief after the death of someone close. This can bring shock, sadness, anger, guilt, and longing.

Anticipatory Grief

This arises when someone you love is approaching the end of life — for example, living with a terminal illness. You may feel sadness before the loss has fully come.

Complicated Grief

Sometimes grief becomes prolonged, intense, and stuck, making it hard to function. This is also known as “prolonged grief disorder” and may benefit from professional help.

Child and Infant Loss

Losing a child brings a grief that feels unnatural and unbearable. Parents may struggle with guilt, questions, and isolation. Support exists specifically for this experience (see links below).

Living Losses

Grief isn’t only about death. It can come with the loss of health, a relationship, a home, or even identity. These losses can feel just as real and painful.

Affirmation: “My grief is mine, and it deserves space.”

Why Grief Affects Us So Deeply

Grief activates the same brain circuits as physical pain. That’s why your chest can ache or your stomach can knot. Anxiety and depression often overlap with grief — racing thoughts, loss of motivation, exhaustion, or feeling numb are all common. You are not broken; you are human.

Practical Supports

  • Cruse Bereavement Support – national UK service
  • Marie Curie Bereavement Support
  • Child Bereavement UK
  • Samaritans (24/7)
  • Breathing Space Scotland – support for low mood, depression, and grief
  • SAMH (Scottish Association for Mental Health)

Affirmation: “Reaching for support is a strength, not a weakness.”

Disclaimer

This page offers general information and supportive resources. It is not a substitute for professional help. If you are struggling to cope with grief and it affects your ability to function, please consider reaching out to a counsellor, therapist, or a trusted health professional.
