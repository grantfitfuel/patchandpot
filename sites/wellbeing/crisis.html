Crisis Support

If everything feels too much right now, you’re not broken — you’re overwhelmed. You are not alone. This page gives you fast options for **right now**, simple tools to steady your body, and places to get confidential support in the UK and Scotland.

1) If you are in immediate danger

  • Call 999 (UK emergency services).
  • If safe, go to your nearest A&E.
  • Call Samaritans 116 123 (free, 24/7, UK & ROI).
  • If speaking is not possible: from a mobile call 999 and follow the **Silent Solution** prompts (you may be asked to press 55 to confirm you need help).

2) If you’re struggling but not in immediate danger

If you feel unsafe but not at immediate risk, these services can help you stay steady and get support:

  • Samaritans — 24/7 listening support (phone, email).
  • Breathing Space (Scotland) — 6pm–2am weekdays, 24/7 weekends.
  • Shout — text SHOUT to 85258 (UK, 24/7 textline).
  • Mind — mental health information & signposting (England & Wales).
  • SANEline — support for people affected by mental illness.
  • NHS urgent mental health helplines (England) — 24/7 local teams.
  • SAMH — mental health information (Scotland).
  • CALM — helpline & webchat (suicide prevention).
  • PAPYRUS HOPELINE247 — under 35s: call 0800 068 4141 / text 07860 039967.

3) Why crisis happens (anxiety, depression & overwhelm)

Crisis is a **body response** to overload. Three common states can show up:

  • Anxiety / panic (fight–flight): racing heart, tight chest, spiralling thoughts. Your sympathetic system is flooding you with adrenaline to “escape”.
  • Depression / shutdown (freeze–collapse): heavy, numb, blank mind, impossible to move. Your body is conserving energy when it feels there’s no way out.
  • Yo-yo: swinging between the two — frantic one moment, flat the next. This is common when you’ve been coping for a long time.

None of this is weakness. It’s biology. If we can settle the body a notch, the mind has more room to think.

4) Grounding tools you can try now

These won’t fix everything, but they can help the next 5–10 minutes feel survivable.

Breathing 4–6

Inhale for 4, exhale for 6 (ten rounds). Longer exhales engage the body’s calming system.

5–4–3–2–1 senses

Notice 5 things you see · 4 touch · 3 hear · 2 smell · 1 taste. Say them out loud if you can.

Temperature & movement

Cool your face/neck with water; press feet into the floor; roll shoulders; swing arms gently. This burns off adrenaline.

One safe contact

Text: “It’s heavy today — can you check in at 7?” Name one feeling and one thing you’ll try (water, fresh air, food).

5) Make a simple safety plan

Write this in your notes app (or on paper) so it’s easy to find:

  • My signs: how I know I’m slipping (not sleeping, racing thoughts, numb, can’t eat).
  • My grounding: 4–6 breathing, 5-4-3-2-1, cold water splash, walk to the nearest tree.
  • My people: name + number (friend, family, helpline).
  • My safe places: a room, a friend’s house, a public space you trust.
  • My reasons: photos or sentences that remind me who/what I care about.

6) Technology & privacy

  • Use a private/incognito window for sensitive searches.
  • Clear recent history; log out of shared accounts.
  • If someone monitors your device, try a library computer or a trusted friend’s phone.
  • Avoid saving helpline numbers under obvious names if safety is a concern.

7) Helplines & services (UK & Scotland)

  • Samaritans — 24/7 free listening (UK & ROI), call 116 123.
  • Breathing Space (Scotland) — evening & weekend support.
  • Shout — text SHOUT to 85258 (24/7 crisis textline).
  • NHS urgent mental health helplines (England).
  • Mind — mental health information & signposting.
  • SANEline — support for people affected by mental illness.
  • SAMH — mental health information (Scotland).
  • CALM — helpline & webchat (suicide prevention).
  • PAPYRUS HOPELINE247 — under 35s: 0800 068 4141 / text 07860 039967.

Important Note

This page is for supportive information only. It’s not a substitute for professional medical, psychological, or crisis care. If you feel unable to keep yourself safe — or someone else is at risk — call 999 (UK) now. If outside the UK, use your local emergency number.

For non-emergency concerns, contact a qualified professional or one of the services listed above.
