Understanding Anxiety

Anxiety is your body’s threat system trying to keep you safe. When it’s on too often or too loud, life shrinks. Here you’ll find clear explanations (the biology), everyday tools that help now, and routes to support in the UK and Scotland.

1) What anxiety feels like (and why it’s confusing)

  • Body: heart racing, tight chest/throat, shaky, hot/cold, GI upset, dizziness.
  • Mind: “what if” loops, catastrophising, blanking out, irritability.
  • Behaviour: avoiding, checking, reassurance seeking, cancelling plans, scrolling.

None of this means you’re broken. It means your alarm is set too sensitive — and sensitivity can be trained.

2) Biology / How it happens

Key systems at a glance

  • Amygdala (alarm) & PFC (brakes): The amygdala flags potential threat fast. The prefrontal cortex (PFC) weighs evidence and applies the brakes. Chronic stress can bias “alarm > brakes.”
  • Locus coeruleus & norepinephrine: Controls arousal/vigilance. Too much norepinephrine = jittery, hyper-alert, sleep-frayed.
  • GABA & glutamate: GABA calms; glutamate excites. Lower GABA tone or higher glutamate can feel like “can’t switch off.”
  • Serotonin: Modulates mood/anxiety, gut-brain signalling, sleep. SSRIs/SNRIs often help by improving signalling.
  • HPA axis & cortisol: The stress-hormone loop. Frequent alarms keep cortisol elevated, disrupting sleep, focus, and digestion.
  • Interoception & CO₂: The body’s sense of internal signals. Low CO₂ tolerance (from chronic over-breathing) makes normal sensations feel alarming; training slower breathing raises the threshold.

Anxiety is multi-factor: genetics, learning history, trauma, sleep, caffeine/alcohol, hormones (thyroid, perimenopause), and life stress all interact.

3) Common anxiety types (quick map)

  • GAD (generalised): Worry about many domains (health, work, finances) most days for months.
  • Panic: Sudden surges of intense fear + body symptoms; fear of the next attack keeps the loop going.
  • Social anxiety: Fear of judgement/embarrassment; avoidance of social/performance situations.
  • Specific phobias: Focused fears (flying, needles, dogs, heights) → strong avoidant patterns.
  • Health anxiety: Misinterpreting bodily sensations as serious illness; repeated checking/searching.
  • OCD (intrusions & compulsions): Repetitive thoughts/urges + ritual behaviours to reduce anxiety.
  • PTSD/trauma-related: Re-experiencing, hyperarousal, avoidance (see Trauma page when ready).

4) Day-to-day tools (fast relief, real skills)

4–6 breathing

Inhale 4s, exhale 6s for 1–3 minutes. Longer exhale nudges the vagus nerve and lowers arousal. If dizzy, slow down; nasal breathing if possible.

Physiological sigh

Two short inhales through the nose + long sigh out the mouth, 1–3 times. Good for quick spikes.

5–4–3–2–1 grounding

5 see, 4 touch, 3 hear, 2 smell, 1 taste. Name each slowly. Brings attention out of the whirlpool.

Temperature & posture

Cool splash wrists/face; open posture; relax jaw/shoulders. Small signals shift state.

Worry window

Park worries to a 15-min daily slot. Outside the slot, note it and return to the moment.

Micro-plan

What’s the smallest helpful action? Set 2–5 min timer. Start ugly, not perfect.

5) Exposure ladders (retrain the alarm)

  1. List steps: 0–10 scale from easiest to hardest (e.g., look at a lift → stand near → ride 1 floor).
  2. Tiny reps: Stay with each step until anxiety peaks and falls (usually minutes). Don’t escape.
  3. Repeat: Come back another day. Consistency trains the amygdala better than heroics.

For trauma-related triggers, work with a professional — graded exposure is powerful but should be safe and consent-led.

6) Thinking tools (language that helps)

From: “This is dangerous.”
To: “This is uncomfortable, not unsafe.”
From: “I can’t cope.”
To: “I can ride this wave for one minute.”
From: “What if…?”
To: “What’s the smallest helpful action?”

7) Lifestyle supports (small dials that matter)

  • Sleep: regular wake time, morning light, dim evenings; phone out of reach at night.
  • Caffeine & alcohol: Reduce caffeine (especially after noon); alcohol rebounds worsen sleep/anxiety next day.
  • Movement: 10–20 mins daily any pace; short “movement snacks” reduce baseline arousal.
  • Food: protein + complex carbs steady energy; don’t skip meals — low blood sugar mimics anxiety.
  • Connection: brief honest check-ins; co-regulation calms the nervous system.

8) When to get help

  • Anxiety is stopping you from work/study/caring for yourself
  • Frequent panic attacks or you’re avoiding many situations
  • Sleep is severely disrupted for weeks
  • You’re using alcohol/drugs to cope
  • Thoughts of self-harm — seek urgent help now (see below)

Effective treatments include CBT (with exposure), ACT, medication (SSRIs/SNRIs, occasionally others), and group/peer support.

9) UK & Scotland – Where to find support

  • Samaritans — 24/7 free listening (UK & ROI: 116 123).
  • Breathing Space (Scotland) — 6pm–2am weekdays, 24/7 weekends.
  • NHS Inform Scotland — mental health self-help & services.
  • NHS Urgent Mental Health helplines (England) — 24/7 local lines.
  • Mind — information & local services in England & Wales.
  • Shout — UK 24/7 text support: text SHOUT to 85258.

Important Note

The information on this page is for general understanding and support. It is not a substitute for professional medical, psychological, or legal advice. If you feel unable to keep yourself safe or someone else is at risk, call 999 (UK) immediately. If you’re outside the UK, contact your local emergency number.

For non-emergency concerns, speak with your GP or a qualified mental health professional, or use the services listed above.
