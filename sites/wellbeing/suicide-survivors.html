After a Suicide Attempt (Survivors & Supporters)

Surviving an attempt can stir relief, fear, shame, anger, and questions — sometimes all in one day. This page offers immediate safety steps, practical tools for the next days and weeks, and clear guidance for the people supporting you. You are not alone.

1) If you need urgent help

  • Call 999 (UK) if there is immediate danger.
  • Samaritans — call 116 123 (free, 24/7, UK & ROI).
  • Shout — text SHOUT to 85258 (free, 24/7 crisis textline, UK).

Save these in your phone favourites and on paper.

2) The first 72 hours: small, protective steps

Medical follow-up

Attend any hospital checks and book a GP appointment. Ask about local crisis teams and short-term follow-up.

Reduce immediate risk

Store or remove means where possible (medications in a lockbox; limit large quantities; secure sharp items if relevant). Share this task with a trusted person.

One or two safe people

Choose people who can listen without judgement. Agree when/how to check in (e.g., morning & evening texts).

Micro-routine

Food, fluids, meds, light movement, outside light. “Good enough” is enough for now.

3) Create a written safety plan

A clear, simple plan helps you and supporters respond fast when risk rises.

  • Warning signs: thoughts, body feelings, situations.
  • Coping steps (1–3): breath (4-6), grounding (5-4-3-2-1), shower/step outside, call a person.
  • Contacts: two safe people + professional & crisis numbers.
  • Safe spaces: places at home/outside you can go.
  • Means safety: specific steps to reduce access when risk rises.

Keep one copy on paper (wallet) and one on your phone. Share with supporters.

4) Stabilise the basics (biology first)

  • Medication: take exactly as prescribed. Report side effects early.
  • Sleep: consistent wind-down; dim light; if awake >20 min, move to a calm room until sleepy.
  • Food & hydration: small frequent meals/snacks; keep easy options (soup, nuts, yoghurt, toast).
  • Light & movement: morning daylight; 5–10 min walk or stretch daily.
  • Alcohol & substances: reduce/avoid — they can worsen low mood and risk.

5) Guidance for supporters (family & friends)

How to talk

  • “I’m glad you’re here. I want to help, and I’ll follow your plan.”
  • Ask open questions: “What might help for the next hour?”
  • Avoid interrogation or blame; focus on safety and care.

Shared plan

  • Keep the safety plan accessible to both of you.
  • Agree simple check-ins (e.g., morning/evening).
  • Divide practical tasks (calls, prescriptions, food shop).

Boundaries & rest

  • Share care with others; use respite if available.
  • Supporters also need sleep, food, and breaks.
  • Seek your own support or peer group.

6) Therapies & treatments (what to expect)

  • CBT/ACT: skills for mood, coping, and unhelpful thought patterns.
  • DBT: skills for emotion regulation and crisis tolerance (helpful if self-harm urges are present).
  • Trauma-focused therapy (TF-CBT, EMDR): if trauma or PTSD is relevant.
  • Medication: your GP/psychiatrist may suggest SSRIs/SNRIs, mood stabilisers, or other options; review regularly.
  • Peer support: groups and helplines reduce isolation and provide practical wisdom.

7) Work, study & daily life

  • Phased return: reduced hours or duties; one point-of-contact.
  • Adjustments: flexible deadlines, quiet space for calls, protected breaks.
  • Script: “I’m receiving medical support. I’d value a phased plan and a single contact.”

8) Relapse prevention (staying safe over time)

  1. Review your safety plan monthly; update warning signs and coping steps.
  2. Schedule routine check-ins (supporter + GP/therapist).
  3. Keep means-safety habits (e.g., weekly pill organiser, limited supplies at home).
  4. Track early dips (sleep, appetite, isolating); act early (extra support, GP call).
  5. Build meaning & connection: small goals, volunteering, creative time, nature.

9) UK & Scotland support

Crisis & listening (24/7)

  • Samaritans — 116 123 (free, 24/7, UK & ROI)
  • Shout — text SHOUT to 85258 (UK 24/7)
  • PAPYRUS (under 35) — HOPELINE247 0800 068 4141; text 07860 039 967

Ongoing support

  • Mind — info & local services
  • Rethink Mental Illness — advice & support groups
  • NHS talking therapies (via your GP)

Scotland

  • Breathing Space Scotland — evenings & weekends
  • NHS Inform (Scotland) — local services & crisis teams

For forces & emergency responders

  • Combat Stress — veterans’ mental health
  • NHS Veterans (Op COURAGE) — veterans’ services (England)
  • Police Care UK — police & families
  • The Fire Fighters Charity — fire & rescue
  • TASC — The Ambulance Staff Charity

For local NHS crisis teams, contact your GP practice or check your local NHS site.

Important Note

The information on this page is for general understanding and support. It is not a substitute for professional medical, psychological, or legal advice. If you feel unable to keep yourself safe or someone else is at risk, call 999 (UK) immediately. If you’re outside the UK, contact your local emergency number.

For non-emergency concerns, consider speaking with a qualified health professional or one of the support services listed above.
