Neurodiversity

Neurodiversity recognises natural differences in how brains process information, sense the world, and communicate. This page focuses on practical support, strengths, and adjustments that can make daily life easier at home, work, and in the community.

1. What neurodiversity means

“Neurodiversity” is an umbrella term that includes profiles such as autism, ADHD, dyslexia, dyspraxia/DCD, and Tourette’s. People may be multiply-neurodivergent, and profiles can overlap. A strengths-based approach asks: what helps this person thrive?

2. Common profiles (very brief)

Autism

Social communication differences, sensory sensitivities, intense interests, need for predictability. Strengths may include deep focus, pattern recognition, honesty, specialist knowledge.

ADHD

Differences in attention regulation, time perception, impulsivity, hyperfocus, and energy levels. Strengths include creativity, rapid problem-solving, enthusiasm, resilience.

Dyslexia

Differences in reading, spelling, and phonological processing; often strong in spatial reasoning, big-picture thinking, empathy, and story telling.

Dyspraxia / DCD

Motor coordination, sequencing, and planning differences; strengths in persistence, empathy, and inventive work-arounds.

Tourette’s

Motor and vocal tics that can fluctuate with stress or fatigue; strengths may include creativity, humour, and determination.

3. Sensory strategies that help

Adjusting sensory load can reduce overwhelm and shutdown. Try:

  • Sound: noise-reducing headphones, soft background sound, leaving noisy spaces early.
  • Light: warmer bulbs, avoiding flicker, daylight breaks.
  • Touch/clothing: soft seams, remove scratchy labels, breathable fabrics.
  • Smell/taste: carry a familiar scent or mint; bland options on tough days.
  • Movement: regulate with short walks, stretching, rocking, or fidget tools.

4. Executive function supports

Executive functions manage planning, starting, shifting, and finishing tasks. Supports reduce friction:

  • Externalise time: visual timers, calendar blocks, alarms.
  • Reduce task size: define a 2–5 minute “first step.”
  • Body-double: work alongside someone (in person or on video) to start.
  • Environment: one “clear zone” on a desk; baskets for “in-progress,” “to file,” “done.”
  • Automate: saved lists, pinned tabs, recurring reminders.

5. School, university, and work adjustments

Adjustments don’t need to be expensive to be effective:

  • Clear written instructions; provide examples and checklists.
  • Flexible deadlines for health/sensory flare-ups; split long meetings.
  • Quiet workspace options; “camera-off” allowances where possible.
  • Alternative communication channels (chat/email) instead of phone-only.
  • Strengths-based role shaping: more deep-work, less context-switching.

6. Masking and burnout

Masking is the effort to hide differences to fit expectations. Over time it can lead to exhaustion, anxiety, depression, and identity loss. Helpful steps:

  • Schedule decompression time after high-demand situations.
  • Limit back-to-back commitments; protect recovery days.
  • Practice “low-mask” time with safe people/places.
  • Use scripts: “I need a moment to think” / “Email works best for me.”
