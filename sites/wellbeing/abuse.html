Support for Those Experiencing Abuse

Abuse can take many forms — physical, emotional, psychological, financial, or coercive control. No one deserves to live in fear or under threat. This page provides explanation, support, and practical steps, alongside trusted links to organisations that can help.

1. Understanding Abuse

Abuse is about power and control. It can happen in relationships, families, or institutions. It may include:

  • Physical abuse: hitting, pushing, restraining, or physical harm.
  • Emotional/psychological abuse: insults, threats, manipulation, gaslighting.
  • Financial abuse: controlling money, denying access to finances, creating dependency.
  • Coercive control: isolating someone, monitoring daily activities, using intimidation.
  • Sexual abuse: any non-consensual sexual contact or pressure.

Recognising abuse is the first step. It is not your fault.

2. Why It Feels So Hard to Leave

Abuse can make people feel trapped because of fear, financial dependency, social pressure, or the hope that things will improve. The psychological impact can include depression, anxiety, trauma responses, or feelings of guilt and shame. None of these mean you are weak — they are common reactions to being controlled and harmed.

Important: If you are reading this and feeling that leaving is impossible, please know there are safe ways to plan and people who understand what you are going through.

3. Steps You Can Take

  • Talk to someone you trust: a friend, family member, or colleague who can listen without judgement.
  • Contact a helpline: confidential services are available 24/7.
  • Make a safety plan: keep important documents, medication, and emergency contacts in a safe place.
  • Use technology safely: clear browsing history, use a private device if possible.

4. Support Services

If you are in immediate danger, call 999 (in the UK).

  • National Domestic Abuse Helpline (0808 2000 247)
  • Refuge – support for women and children.
  • Men’s Advice Line – support for men experiencing abuse.
  • Scottish Women’s Aid – support across Scotland.
  • Safer.Scot – information and support for people in Scotland.
  • NHS Inform Scotland – guidance on abuse and where to get help.
  • Childline (0800 1111) – for children and young people.
  • Hourglass (formerly Action on Elder Abuse) – for older people.

5. For Friends and Family

If you suspect someone you care about is experiencing abuse:

  • Listen and believe them.
  • Do not pressure them to act before they are ready.
  • Help them know about safe services and hotlines.
  • Check in regularly and offer small, practical support (e.g. a safe lift, storing documents).

Disclaimer: This page is for general information and signposting only. It is not a substitute for professional medical, legal, or crisis support. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. If you need confidential support, please use the services listed above.
