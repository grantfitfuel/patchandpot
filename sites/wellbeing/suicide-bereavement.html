Suicide Bereavement

The death of someone you care about by suicide is a uniquely painful kind of grief. Alongside shock and sadness, you may face guilt, anger, confusion, or unanswered questions. This page offers explanations, gentle coping tools, and links to specialised UK and Scottish support.

1) How suicide bereavement feels

  • Shock: the suddenness can leave you numb or unable to believe it’s real.
  • Guilt: thoughts of “what if” or “I should have…” are common but not your fault.
  • Anger: towards the person, services, yourself, or the world.
  • Stigma & isolation: people may avoid talking, leaving you more alone.
  • Physical strain: disrupted sleep, appetite, concentration, or immune health.

Every response is valid — there is no “right way” to grieve.

2) What makes suicide bereavement different

Research shows bereavement by suicide carries higher risk of trauma symptoms, complicated grief, and social isolation compared to other types of loss.

  • Unanswered questions: you may never fully know “why.”
  • Trauma overlap: some experience flashbacks of the event or discovery.
  • Fear of judgement: worry about how others see your family or loved one.
  • Ripple effects: colleagues, classmates, and whole communities may be deeply impacted.

3) Early days & first steps

  • Let practical support in: meals, childcare, admin. Others often want to help — be specific if you can.
  • Contact a bereavement support service early — they understand this unique grief.
  • Take rest where possible; sleep disruption is common but small routines help.
  • Allow emotions to come in waves — shock, numbness, and sudden floods are all normal.

Funeral and inquest processes may feel overwhelming. Bereavement organisations can guide you step by step.

4) Supporting children & young people

Children need age-appropriate, honest explanations. Avoid euphemisms (“gone to sleep”) which can confuse.

  • Simple truth: “They died because their brain was very unwell.”
  • Reassure safety: “It wasn’t your fault. You are cared for.”
  • Invite questions: children may revisit the loss at different stages of development.
  • School link: inform teachers so they can provide support.

5) Long-term grief & anniversaries

Grief after suicide can last years and resurface at anniversaries, birthdays, or life milestones. Healing often looks like learning to carry the loss rather than “getting over it.”

  • Anniversaries may trigger strong emotions — plan gentle routines and supportive company.
  • Peer groups can reduce isolation and offer shared understanding.
  • Memorial rituals — lighting a candle, planting a tree — can bring comfort.

6) Workplace & community impact

Suicide loss affects more than immediate family. Friends, colleagues, teams, and whole communities may struggle with guilt, blame, or shock. Workplaces and schools should consider postvention support:

  • Clear, compassionate communication.
  • Time off or adjustments for those most affected.
  • Group support or facilitated debriefs.
  • Access to professional bereavement counsellors.

7) Support services (UK & Scotland)

UK-wide

  • Samaritans — 116 123 (24/7 listening)
  • Survivors of Bereavement by Suicide (SoBS) — peer groups & helpline
  • Cruse Bereavement Support — helpline & counselling
  • Mind — resources and guides

Scotland

  • Support After Suicide Scotland — local peer groups & resources
  • Breathing Space Scotland — 0800 83 85 87, evenings & weekends
  • NHS Inform Scotland — bereavement and mental health services

If you’re outside the UK, check local suicide bereavement charities or crisis lines.

Important Note

The information on this page is for general understanding and support. It is not a substitute for professional medical, psychological, or legal advice. If you feel unable to keep yourself safe or someone else is at risk, call 999 (UK) immediately. If you’re outside the UK, contact your local emergency number.

For non-emergency concerns, consider speaking with a qualified health professional or one of the bereavement services listed above.
