Understanding Grief

Grief isn’t something to “get over.” It’s love trying to find a new shape. If you feel foggy, overwhelmed, numb, angry, guilty, or suddenly okay and then not — you’re not broken, you’re human. This page explains grief in everyday language and points you to real help.

1. Types of Grief

Grief has many shapes. You might recognise more than one, or move between them over time.

Anticipatory grief

Begins before a death or major loss (e.g., terminal illness, dementia). You grieve “little losses” — changes in memory, roles, or independence — while still showing up with love. Mixed feelings (sadness, tenderness, guilt, relief) are normal.

Complicated / prolonged grief

Grief that stays intense and disruptive for a long time. It’s more common after sudden or traumatic loss or when there were unresolved conflicts. Signs include persistent yearning, feeling life can’t move forward, or avoiding reminders. Specialist support can really help.

Disenfranchised grief

Pain that isn’t recognised by others — miscarriage, suicide, pet loss, estrangement, ending a relationship. The loss is real, but validation is missing. Finding people who “get it” lightens the load.

Collective grief

Communities grieving together after disaster, pandemics, or public tragedies. It can feel heavy and strangely unifying at once.

Secondary losses

We grieve routines, roles, places, and identity (e.g., “the person who made me feel like someone’s child”). These hidden losses often explain why grief returns in waves.

Childhood grief

Children grieve in bursts — playing one moment, deeply sad the next. Repeating questions and changes in behaviour are common. Gentle honesty and steady presence matter more than perfect words.

2. Why Grief Feels Overwhelming

Grief affects the whole body, not just emotions:

  • Brain fog: forgetfulness, poor focus, feeling “spaced out”.
  • Body load: heavy tiredness, aches, disrupted sleep and appetite.
  • Emotional waves: sadness, anger, guilt, relief, numbness — all can be normal.
  • Social strain: feeling out of step with others, withdrawing, not wanting to explain.

3. Sarah’s Story

“When my mum died, I thought I had to ‘be strong’ and go back to normal fast. But normal had changed. Some days I forgot to eat; other days I laughed at tiny things and then felt guilty. I thought I was doing grief wrong until a support group told me: ‘Everyone’s rhythm is different.’ It didn’t fix the pain, but it let me breathe.”

Stories like Sarah’s remind us there’s no timetable. If your grief looks different to someone else’s, that’s okay.

4. Anxiety & Depression in Grief

Grief and mental health often overlap. Anxiety can make you restless, fearful of the future, or flooded by “what ifs.” Depression can bring emptiness, loss of motivation, or hopeless thoughts. These experiences don’t mean you’re failing at grief — they signal that your brain and body are under strain and might need extra support.

If anxiety or depression makes daily life unmanageable for weeks, or you’re having thoughts of not wanting to be here, please reach out for professional help or a helpline listed below.

5. What Helps Day-to-Day

Tiny routines
Anchor the day with one repeatable act (cup of tea outside, short walk, water a plant). Predictability soothes.
Breathing
In 4, out 6 — two minutes. It shifts your nervous system toward calm.
Names & dates
Say their name. Mark “griefy” days gently. Plan support around anniversaries.
Permission
You can cry, laugh, be angry, be numb. Wave, not ladder.
Connection
One human who listens beats ten perfect phrases. Text someone safe: “Can I send a messy thought?”
Body care
Food, water, meds, sleep. Basics first; mood follows.

6. Where to Find Support

You don’t have to carry grief alone. These trusted services can help:

  • Cruse Bereavement Support — national helpline & local groups.
  • Child Bereavement UK — support for children and families.
  • SANDS — stillbirth & neonatal loss support.
  • Samaritans — 24/7 listening (UK: 116 123).
  • NHS Inform (Scotland) — up-to-date regional bereavement services via your health board.

In immediate danger or unable to stay safe: call emergency services.

7. Disclaimer

This page offers information and support only. It isn’t a substitute for professional medical advice, therapy, or crisis care. If your grief, anxiety, or depression makes day-to-day life unmanageable, please seek professional help or contact one of the helplines above.
