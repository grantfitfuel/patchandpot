Grief Support

Grief is not a problem to fix — it is the cost of love. It affects mind, body, and daily life in ways that can feel overwhelming. This page explains what grief does, why it feels so disorienting, and where to find support in Scotland and across the UK.

What grief really is

Grief is your brain and body adjusting to the absence of someone (or something) important. It is not only sadness — it can be anger, numbness, relief, longing, guilt, or disbelief. These emotions often shift hour by hour.

  • Emotional impact: sadness, anxiety, irritability, or even moments of laughter — all are part of grieving.
  • Physical impact: exhaustion, disrupted sleep, loss of appetite, heaviness in the chest, memory gaps.
  • Cognitive impact: “grief fog” — struggling to concentrate, forgetting simple tasks.
  • Spiritual/meaning impact: questioning beliefs, searching for “why.”

Why grief feels so consuming

When someone dies (or we anticipate their death), the brain’s attachment system struggles. Pathways built over years expect them to walk in, call, or laugh again. Each reminder triggers stress responses:

  • Sympathetic arousal: anxiety, racing heart, panic — the nervous system “searching” for the person.
  • Parasympathetic shutdown: exhaustion, flatness, not wanting to get out of bed.
  • Depressive overlay: hopelessness, loss of meaning, belief “I’ll never feel joy again.”

Ways to cope day-to-day

There is no timetable for grief. What helps is not “getting over it,” but learning to live alongside it.

  • Anchor routines: small daily rituals provide structure.
  • Breath and body: slow exhalations, grounding with hands on a surface.
  • Memory keeping: photos, journals, or a “grief box.”
  • Permission for joy: moments of laughter are part of healing.
  • Connection: talking, texting, or sitting with someone safe.
  • Practical help: allow others to cook, shop, or do admin tasks.
