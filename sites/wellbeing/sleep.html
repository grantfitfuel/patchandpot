Sleep & Insomnia

Sleep isn’t a luxury — it’s a daily reset for mood, memory, immunity and energy. When insomnia takes hold, nights feel endless and days feel foggy. This guide explains how sleep works, why insomnia hangs on, and the most effective tools (including CBT-I) to reclaim your nights — kindly and realistically.

1) Why Sleep Matters (what it does for you)

  • Memory & learning: Deep sleep stabilises new facts; REM links ideas and tunes emotional tone.
  • Mood regulation: Sleep trims threat bias, reducing anxiety & irritability the next day.
  • Body repair: Hormones for growth, immune function and appetite balance reset overnight.
  • Pain & inflammation: Better sleep lowers pain sensitivity and inflammatory signals.

2) Why Insomnia Happens

  • Hyperarousal: stress, pain, or anxious thinking keep the “threat system” lit, making sleep elusive.
  • Conditioning: after rough nights, bed becomes linked with effort and alertness (learned insomnia).
  • Body clock drift: late light/screens & irregular wake times move your rhythm out of sync.
  • Substances: caffeine (up to 12h), alcohol (fragments sleep), nicotine (stimulant).
  • Health factors: pain, reflux, menopause, ADHD, PTSD, sleep apnoea — all can tangle sleep.

3) Types of Insomnia

  • Onset: trouble falling asleep (>30 minutes most nights).
  • Maintenance: waking through the night and struggling to resettle.
  • Early-morning: waking too early and can’t return to sleep.
  • Acute vs chronic: <3 months often stress-related; ≥3 months often needs structured CBT-I.

4) Tonight’s Toolkit (gentle, doable)

Wind-down (20–45 min)

  • Dim lights; lower screen brightness or audio-only.
  • Slow task: shower, light stretch, prepare clothes.
  • Ritual: brew caffeine-free tea; same mug, same chair.

Breath & body

  • In 4, out 6 for 2–3 minutes (long exhale = calm signal).
  • Progressive muscle relax: tense–release feet→face.

Worry container

  • Two-column jot: “worries → next tiny step tomorrow”.
  • Close the notebook; promise to revisit after breakfast.

Temperature & light

  • Cool, dark, quiet (16–18°C). Consider eye mask/earplugs.
  • Phones outside bedroom or >2m away on Do Not Disturb.

5) Core CBT-I Techniques (gold standard)

Stimulus Control (re-train the bed)

  • Use bed for sleep and sex only (no doomscrolling, emails, TV).
  • If not asleep in ~20 minutes (don’t clock-watch): get up to a quiet, dim room; return when drowsy.
  • Fixed wake time every day (weekends too) — anchor your body clock.
  • Avoid daytime naps in week 1–2 (unless safety-critical, then keep it <20 min, before 3 pm).

Sleep Restriction → Sleep Scheduling (build sleep drive)

  1. Track 1 week with a sleep diary → estimate average sleep (e.g., 5h 45m).
  2. Set a fixed wake (e.g., 07:00). Time-in-bed = average sleep + ~30–45m (min ~6h).
  3. Hold this schedule nightly for a week. Expect a bit of sleepiness early on — that’s sleep drive building.
  4. When sleep efficiency >85% for 5–7 nights, add 15 minutes to time-in-bed. Repeat weekly.

Safety: Not for untreated sleep apnoea, certain bipolar conditions, seizure disorders, or if drowsy driving risk. If unsure, speak to your GP first.

Cognitive tools

  • “I need 8 hours or I’m ruined” → “I can function on ‘good enough’ while I retrain sleep.”
  • “It’s 2 am — disaster” → “Clocks lie at night. I’ll run the plan.”

Relaxation set

  • Box breath (4-4-4-4) × 3 minutes.
  • Guided body scan (audio), or “count breaths” down from 100.

Light & timing

  • Morning daylight (5–20 min) to anchor the clock.
  • Lower light 60–90 min pre-bed. Warm-tone lamps help.

6) Daytime Habits That Pay Off at Night

  • Movement: any daily movement improves depth of sleep; avoid hard sessions within 2–3h of bed if it revs you up.
  • Caffeine: last cup 8–10 hours before bed (earlier if sensitive).
  • Alcohol: may help you doze, but fragments sleep and REM; aim for alcohol-free nights during CBT-I.
  • Nicotine: stimulating; avoid close to bedtime.
  • Naps: keep to 10–20 minutes, before 3 pm (or skip while rebuilding sleep pressure).

7) Special Situations

Shift work

  • Protect a 90-minute pre-sleep wind-down even in daytime (dark curtains, eye mask).
  • Post-shift: sunglasses outside; short nap strategy before night shifts if needed.

Teens & young adults

  • Body clock runs later; target gradual shifts (15 min earlier every 2–3 nights).
  • Morning light + consistent wake time are the biggest levers.

Menopause/perimenopause

  • Keep room cool; breathable bedding; consider layered clothing.
  • Talk to GP about HRT options if symptoms are severe.

Chronic pain

  • Positioning (pillows), gentle stretch/heat before bed, paced-day approach.
  • CBT-I still works; adapt timing to pain rhythms.

PTSD & nightmares

  • Grounding before bed; reduce triggers in the sleep space.
  • Ask about Imagery Rehearsal Therapy (rewrite the dream script and practise while awake).

Possible sleep apnoea

  • Loud snoring, witnessed pauses, morning headaches, daytime sleepiness — speak to your GP about assessment.

8) When to Seek More Help (UK & Scotland)

  • NHS Inform Scotland — Sleep
  • Mind — Sleep problems
  • Breathing Space Scotland (evenings & weekends)
  • NHS — Every Mind Matters: Sleep
  • Samaritans — free 24/7 listening (UK: 116 123)

If low mood, anxiety or distress escalate — especially thoughts of self-harm — contact urgent support (Samaritans or NHS urgent mental health helpline) or call 999 in an emergency.

Important Note

The information on this page is for general understanding and support. It is not a substitute for professional medical, psychological, or legal advice.
