Support for People Experiencing Abuse

If you’re reading this, you may be living with fear, control, or harm. You’re not overreacting, you’re not to blame, and you’re not alone. This page explains what abuse can look like day-to-day, why it’s so hard to leave, how to make a plan, and where to get help across the UK and Scotland.

1. Understanding Abuse (what it looks like day-to-day)

Abuse is about power and control. It can be physical, emotional, sexual, financial, or involve coercive control — patterns of isolation, monitoring, intimidation, or rules that make you smaller and more afraid over time.

  • Physical: pushing, restraining, hitting, blocking doors, destroying property.
  • Emotional/psychological: insults, threats, humiliation, gaslighting, silent treatment, social isolation.
  • Coercive control: tracking your phone, policing clothes or friends, controlling food/sleep, interrogations.
  • Sexual: pressure, coercion, or any sexual activity without enthusiastic consent.
  • Financial: taking your wages/benefits, questioning every purchase, sabotaging work, forcing debt.
  • Digital abuse: reading messages, demanding passwords, spyware, controlling your online life.

If you recognise even a few of these, your experience is valid. Abuse often ramps up slowly — your reactions (fear, freeze, appease) are normal nervous-system responses.

2. Why it feels so hard to leave (you’re not weak — you’re human)

Abusive dynamics reshape your nervous system and beliefs. Many people experience anxiety, depression, trauma responses (fight/flight/freeze/fawn), and practical barriers (money, housing, children, immigration, caring duties).

  • Trauma bond: cycles of fear and affection build powerful attachment.
  • Isolation & self-doubt: “Maybe it’s my fault.” (It isn’t.)
  • Practical traps: finances, visas, housing, disability, caring responsibilities.
  • Escalation worries: danger can increase when leaving — safety planning helps.

None of this means you can’t leave — it means you deserve time, support, and a plan.

3. A simple safety plan (tiny steps count)

Pack & store (if safe)

  • Key documents: ID, benefits letters, prescriptions.
  • Spare keys, cash card, old phone/charger.
  • Essential meds, comfort item for kids.

Tech safety

  • Use a private device when you can; clear history.
  • Change passwords from a safe device; enable 2FA.
  • Turn off location sharing; check apps for access.

Who & where

  • Safe people: 1–2 trusted contacts.
  • Safe places: friend’s house, GP, public space, refuge.
  • Code words for “please call me” or “call 999”.

4. If you’re staying for now

  • Reduce harm: know the higher-risk times/places; keep doors clear; store a phone somewhere safe.
  • Regulate: long exhales, drink water, text a safe person — small resets calm a stressed nervous system.
  • Record: if safe, keep a log (dates, what happened, photos of damage/injuries).
  • Support: speak with helplines for planning even if you’re not ready to leave.

5. After leaving (you may feel mixed — that’s normal)

Relief can sit beside grief, fear, or numbness. Trauma recovery is not linear. Focus on sleep, food, gentle movement, and safe people. Refuges and advocates can help with housing, legal options, and benefits.

6. Support services (UK & Scotland)

If you’re in immediate danger: call 999. If you can’t speak, use the Silent Solution by pressing 55 when prompted.

UK-wide

  • National Domestic Abuse Helpline (24/7, women & children)
  • Men’s Advice Line
  • Galop (LGBTQ+)
  • Victim Support
  • Childline (under 19)
  • Karma Nirvana (honour-based abuse/forced marriage)

Scotland

  • Scottish Women’s Aid
  • Safer.Scot (Scottish Government hub)
  • NHS Inform: Domestic Abuse
  • Rape Crisis Scotland
  • Shared Care Scotland (short breaks/respite)
  • Breathing Space (listening support)

Other useful

  • Refuge
  • Hourglass (older people)
  • SurvivorsUK (men/non-binary, sexual violence)
  • Forced Marriage Unit
  • Shout (24/7 text: SHOUT to 85258)

Important Note

The information on this page is for general understanding and support. It is not a substitute for professional medical, legal, or crisis services. If you feel unable to keep yourself or someone else safe, call 999 (UK) immediately. If you’re outside the UK, contact your local emergency service.

If you’re not in immediate danger but need help, please contact one of the services listed above or a qualified professional.
