Grief

Grief isn’t a straight line. It comes in waves — sometimes big, sometimes small. These tools help you steady yourself, honour what matters, and find simple care on hard days.

The Waves Model

Why it feels better, then worse, then better again.

Think of grief like the sea: waves come and go. You can’t stop them, but you can learn to float. On rough days, shorten the plan, add more rest, and use a calm tool.

  • Big wave: lower expectations, ask for help, keep food and water simple.
  • Small wave: do light tasks, short walk, one message to someone you trust.

Not Linear

You’re not “back at square one”.

Upsurges months or years later are part of the process. Often they’re linked to dates, places, music, smells, or tiredness. Note the trigger; plan one small comfort next time.

Plan around key dates

2-Minute Anchor

Box breathing (4-4-4-4).

  1. Exhale fully
  2. Inhale 4 • Hold 4 • Exhale 4 • Hold 4
  3. Repeat 4–6 cycles

Pairs well with a brief outdoor pause or watering a plant.

Rituals That Help

Small actions that carry meaning.

  • Grow a pot in their honour (name the plant; note first leaves/flowers).
  • Keep a memory notebook: a favourite phrase, a story, a recipe.
  • A weekly walk or cup of tea at a set time; light a battery candle.

Your Grief Toolbox

Pick 2–3 things that work for you.

  • Walk for 10 minutes, or water plants slowly
  • Write 5 lines: what you miss + one thing you’re grateful for
  • Breathing tool + drink of water

Let One Person In

Support lands when people know how to help.

Message a friend: “Today is heavy. A short call or a walk would help.”

Anniversaries & Dates

Plan for the tide to rise.

  • Book something light: a short walk, favourite food, quiet time
  • Invite someone to check in that day
  • Have a simple “opt-out” message ready

Physiology First

Grief is physical too.

  • Hydration before caffeine
  • Simple meals (protein + carbs + fruit/veg)
  • Wind-down window: screens low, lights dim, same time nightly

When to Seek Help

There’s support for the long haul.

  • Persistent inability to function (e.g., self-care, work, caring)
  • Intrusive thoughts or urges to harm yourself
  • Complicated grief after sudden/traumatic loss

For urgent concerns, contact local emergency services or crisis lines.

Anticipatory Grief

Grief that begins before the loss.

  • Name it; it’s normal
  • Shorten plans; accept waves of sadness or anger
  • Capture stories, photos, favourite sayings

Supporting Children

Clear words; repeat often.

  • Use concrete language (“died”) and short sentences
  • Answer questions again later
  • Keep routines where possible; add small memorial activities

Sudden Loss

Shock and numbness are protective.

  • Stick to basics: water, food, rest, simple to-dos
  • Repeat information; write down what’s decided
  • Accept practical help; defer non-urgent choices
