Suicide Bereavement

When someone dies by suicide, grief can feel shocking, disorienting, and unlike any other loss. People often carry love, sorrow, anger, guilt, relief, confusion, and unanswered questions — sometimes all in the same day. This page offers clear explanations, practical ways to cope, and UK/Scotland support options for you and those around you.

1) If you need urgent help

  • Call 999 if you or someone else is at immediate risk (UK).
  • Samaritans — call 116 123 (free, 24/7, UK & ROI).
  • Shout — text SHOUT to 85258 for free 24/7 crisis text support (UK).

You are not alone. Reaching out is an act of care, not a burden.

2) The first days & weeks: what to expect

  • Shock & numbness: disrupted sleep, appetite changes, and dream-like unreality are common.
  • Police & formalities: you may have contact with police and a family liaison officer. Processes differ across the UK; ask questions and request a support person for calls/meetings.
  • Coroner/Procurator Fiscal: in England & Wales there may be a coroner’s inquest; in Scotland, the Procurator Fiscal may investigate and, in some cases, there may be a Fatal Accident Inquiry. These processes can take time and can be emotionally hard. It’s okay to bring someone with you and to request breaks.
  • Media & social media: consider nominating one family spokesperson; ask others not to speculate publicly; report harmful content to platforms.
  • Practical help: ask trusted people to help with calls, meals, lifts, collecting documents, or caring duties.

3) What this grief can feel like

Guilt & self-blame

“I should have known/done more.” Guilt means you cared deeply; it isn’t proof of facts.

Anger

At the person, at systems, at yourself, at the world. Anger is a normal response to pain and shock.

Relief & love

Relief that suffering ended can coexist with grief and love. Mixed feelings are human.

Shame & stigma

Fear of judgements can make people withdraw. You deserve compassion and respect.

Body responses

Chest tightness, stomach upset, fatigue, brain fog — grief is physical as well as emotional.

There’s no right timeline. Grief moves in waves; it often softens with time and support.

4) Why it feels so complex

  • Trauma + loss together: the brain is processing a sudden, painful story and a deep attachment loss at once.
  • Unanswered questions: the mind searches for a “why,” often replaying last moments or conversations.
  • Sense-making & narratives: people may create simple stories to cope. It’s okay if your story is still forming.
  • Secondary losses: identity, roles, routines, finances, and community connections can change overnight.

5) Trauma responses & intrusive images

After a traumatic loss, the brain can replay scenes or create imagined details. You may notice flashbacks, nightmares, startle responses, or avoidance of reminders.

  • Grounding: 5–4–3–2–1 senses; name five things you see, four you feel, three you hear, two you smell, one you taste.
  • Breath: in for 4, out for 6 (2 minutes). Long exhale gently quiets the alarm system.
  • Safe place visual: picture a place you feel safe; rehearse details (light, sound, texture).
  • Containment: write/record a distressing thought, then place it in a box/envelope; return later with support.
  • Nights: reduce late news/social media; low light, warm drink, calming audio; if awake >20 min, move to a calm space and return when drowsy.

If flashbacks/nightmares remain intense for weeks or prevent daily life, ask your GP about trauma-focused therapies (e.g., EMDR, trauma-focused CBT).

6) Day-to-day coping

Tiny anchors

Drink water, eat something simple, step outside. “Good enough” self-care is still care.

Connection

One honest check-in today (message/call/walk). People often want to help but don’t know how; tell them.

Admin pacing

One task a day; accept help with forms/calls. Keep a simple list and cross off small wins.

Memory corner

Photos, letters, objects, music. Continuing bonds are healthy and easing.

Body first

Regular meds, gentle movement, warm showers, and sleep basics support emotional processing.

Peer & professional

Bereavement groups reduce isolation; trauma-aware counselling can help unstick places that feel frozen.

7) Children & young people

  • Clear words: avoid euphemisms (e.g., “died by suicide” rather than “went to sleep”). Short truths are kinder.
  • Questions on repeat: children revisit grief as they grow. Keep answering gently with the same core message.
  • Predictability: simple routines and visual plans lower anxiety.
  • School link: inform school/college; request adjustments and a named contact.
  • Memory rituals: drawings, letters, planting something, small ceremonies.

8) For friends, family & workplaces

What helps

  • “I’m so sorry. I’m here.” (no fixing)
  • Practical support (meals, childcare, lifts, admin)
  • Use the person’s name if the bereaved is comfortable
  • Check in again after the first month and beyond

What to avoid

  • Speculation or blame
  • “At least…” statements
  • Timelines (“You should be over it”)
  • Unasked-for comparisons

Workplace

  • Compassionate leave; flexible/phased return
  • Reduced workload; single point of contact
  • Private space for calls or pauses

9) Practical admin (UK/Scotland)

  • Death registration & certificates: request multiple copies.
  • Coroner (England & Wales) / Procurator Fiscal (Scotland): ask the officer/liaison about timelines, viewing reports, and support at hearings.
  • Tell Us Once (UK): where available, notifies multiple government departments in one go.
  • Finances & estates: banks, utilities, landlords/mortgage providers, insurers; seek free debt/benefits advice if needed.
  • Keep a simple folder: dates, names, phone numbers, copies/photos of documents.

It’s okay to ask a trusted person to sit with you during calls or appointments.

10) Rituals, memorials & online spaces

  • Personal rituals: letters, candles, favourite music/places, cooking their meal.
  • Memorials: small gatherings, charities they cared about, living memorials (plants/trees).
  • Online: moderate memorial pages; set clear boundaries; report harmful content.

11) Anniversaries & triggers plan

  • Lower the bar: keep the day simple; cancel non-essentials.
  • Choose company/quiet: decide what helps this year, not forever.
  • Small ritual: a place visit, candle, letter, playlist, charity action.
  • Exit plan: arrange a “leave early” signal if attending events.

12) When grief remains stuck

If months have passed and you feel permanently “frozen,” unable to imagine a future, avoiding all reminders, or constantly overwhelmed by guilt/anger, consider specialist support for:

  • Prolonged/complicated grief (grief-focused therapy)
  • PTSD/trauma (EMDR, trauma-focused CBT)
  • Depression/anxiety (talking therapies; your GP can advise on options)

13) After a suicide attempt (survivors & supporters)

Surviving an attempt can bring relief, fear, shame, and questions. Recovery is possible with support. Loved ones may feel hyper-vigilant and exhausted. You are a team — pace and honesty matter.

For survivors

  • Medical & safety first: follow up with GP/mental health team; create a written safety plan (warning signs, coping steps, who to contact, safe places).
  • Stabilise basics: meds as prescribed, regular meals, gentle movement, alcohol moderation, sleep routine.
  • Shame & secrecy: choose two safe people to tell; agree what support looks like.
  • Therapies: consider CBT/ACT/DBT/EMDR depending on needs; ask about local NHS or charity options.
  • Digital helpers: safety plan on paper + phone; use crisis numbers in contacts.

For supporters

  • Stay collaborative: agree a simple check-in routine and a shared safety plan.
  • Language: “I’m glad you’re here. When it’s hard, we’ll use the plan.” Avoid monitoring/accusations.
  • Boundaries: support doesn’t mean 24/7 hyper-vigilance; share care where possible.
  • Your wellbeing: seek your own support/peer group; rest and respite are protective.

14) Support & services (UK & Scotland)

24/7 crisis & immediate support

  • Samaritans: 116 123 — free, 24/7 listening (UK & ROI)
  • Shout: text SHOUT to 85258 — 24/7 crisis textline (UK)
  • PAPYRUS (under 35): HOPELINE247 0800 068 4141; text 07860 039 967

Bereavement after suicide

  • Survivors of Bereavement by Suicide (SOBS) — peer groups & support
  • Support After Suicide Partnership — information & signposting
  • Cruse Bereavement Support — helpline & local services
  • Winston’s Wish / Child Bereavement UK — support for children & families

Scotland

  • Breathing Space Scotland — evenings & weekends listening support
  • NHS Inform (Scotland) — local mental health & bereavement services

For forces & emergency responders

  • Combat Stress — veterans’ mental health support
  • NHS Veterans (Op COURAGE) — veterans’ mental health services (England)
  • Police Care UK — support for police & families
  • The Fire Fighters Charity — support for fire service personnel
  • TASC (The Ambulance Staff Charity) — ambulance community support

For local NHS crisis teams or talking therapies, contact your GP practice or search your local NHS site.

Important Note

The information on this page is for general understanding and support. It is not a substitute for professional medical, psychological, or legal advice. If you feel unable to keep yourself safe or someone else is at risk, call 999 (UK) immediately. If you’re outside the UK, contact your local emergency number.

For non-emergency concerns, consider speaking with a qualified health professional or one of the support services listed above.
