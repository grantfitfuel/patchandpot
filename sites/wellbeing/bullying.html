Bullying & Online Safety

Bullying can be in-person, online, or both. It harms confidence, concentration, sleep, and mental health. This page explains what bullying looks like today, how to protect yourself, collect evidence, and where to get help across the UK and Scotland.

1. Recognise Bullying

Bullying is repeated behaviour that intends to hurt, humiliate, or control. It can be:

  • Verbal: insults, threats, slurs, rumours, “jokes” that target you.
  • Social: exclusion, group pile-ons, doxxing, impersonation.
  • Physical: pushing, tripping, damaging belongings.
  • Cyberbullying: DMs, comments, group chats, image-based abuse, deepfakes.

If it’s targeted, repeated, and harms you — it counts. You’re not “too sensitive.”

2. Online Safety: Practical Protections

  • Privacy hygiene: lock down profiles, review followers, disable location tags.
  • Block & filter: block users, mute keywords, restrict comments/DMs.
  • Two-factor authentication: protect accounts; don’t reuse passwords.
  • Image-based safety: report non-consensual images immediately; preserve evidence (see below).
  • Device check: scan for stalkerware, remove unknown logins, review “family sharing.”

3. Keep Evidence (without engaging)

  • Screenshots & screen recordings of messages, usernames, dates and times.
  • Export chat logs where possible; save URLs.
  • Witness notes (who saw it, when).
  • Do not retaliate: it can complicate school/work investigations.
  • Back up to a cloud folder you control; share with a trusted adult or manager if appropriate.

4. Schools & Workplaces: Your Rights

Schools (UK/Scotland)

  • Every school must have an anti-bullying policy. Ask for it and refer to it when reporting.
  • Report to a trusted teacher, guidance/pastoral staff, or the head. Provide evidence and dates.
  • Request a written plan: supervision points, seating changes, monitoring of online issues, safe routes.
  • In Scotland, see Respect for All national approach via your council or school handbook.

Workplaces (UK)

  • Check your employer’s dignity at work / anti-bullying policy.
  • Keep a log; report to line manager or HR; bring a colleague/union rep to meetings.
  • Reasonable adjustments may apply if bullying impacts mental health (Equality Act 2010).

5. Mental Health & Coping

  • Limit exposure windows: check messages at set times, not constantly.
  • Body reset: 4–6 breathing (in 4, out 6 for 2 minutes), short walk, stretch sequence.
  • Self-talk swap: “This is not about my worth. I’m taking the next safe step.”
  • Support loop: tell one safe person; schedule a regular check-in.

See also: Anxiety, Depression, Reframing tools.

6. For Parents & Carers

  • Believe first, problem-solve later. Ask: “What would help you feel safer this week?”
  • Phone plan together: privacy settings, reporting, blocking, time windows.
  • School contact: log each incident; copy the school’s anti-bullying lead/Head of Year.
  • Watch for signs: sleep changes, school refusal, headaches, secrecy about devices.

7. Support & Reporting (UK & Scotland)

  • respectme (Scotland) — national anti-bullying service.
  • Childline (0800 1111) — support for children/young people.
  • NSPCC — advice for parents & reporting concerns.
  • CEOP — report online sexual abuse or exploitation.
  • Samaritans (116 123) — 24/7 listening support.
  • Mind: Bullying & Harassment
  • Scottish resources roundup (council/school links).
  • Platform help centres: report abuse directly in-app (Instagram, TikTok, Snapchat, Discord, WhatsApp, X).

If you are in immediate danger or a crime is occurring, call 999 (UK).

Important Note

The information on this page is for general understanding and support. It is not a substitute for professional medical, legal, or crisis services. If you feel unable to keep yourself or someone else safe, call 999 (UK) immediately. If you’re outside the UK, contact your local emergency service.

For non-emergency concerns, consider speaking with a trusted adult, your GP, school, workplace HR, or one of the support services listed above.
