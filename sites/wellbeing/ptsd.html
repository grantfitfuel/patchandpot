PTSD & Trauma

Post-traumatic stress is a learned survival response after overwhelming events. Flashbacks, hypervigilance, and avoidance are not weakness — they’re your brain’s effort to protect you. This page explains the biology, common symptoms, practical grounding tools, and where to get support (including services for veterans and emergency responders).

1) What is PTSD?

PTSD can follow life-threatening or overwhelming events (assault, abuse, war, accidents, medical trauma, disasters). The alarm system learns that the world is unsafe and stays on high alert. Triggers can be obvious (sirens) or subtle (smells, lighting, body sensations).

  • PTSD: develops after one or more traumatic events.
  • Complex PTSD (cPTSD): often from prolonged or repeated trauma (e.g., childhood abuse, coercive control).

2) Symptoms (the four clusters) & cPTSD

Intrusions

  • Flashbacks, nightmares, intrusive images
  • Startle response; body jolts “out of the blue”

Avoidance

  • Dodging places, people, conversations, smells
  • Shutting down feelings; numbing

Negative mood & thinking

  • Guilt, shame, “I’m broken / it was my fault”
  • Feeling detached, joyless, low self-worth

Arousal & reactivity

  • Hypervigilance, irritability, poor sleep
  • Concentration problems, risk-scan loops

cPTSD additions

  • Emotion regulation difficulties (big swings, shutdown)
  • Negative self-beliefs (“I’m worthless/dangerous”)
  • Relationship/attachment challenges; dissociation

3) Biology: why the alarm won’t switch off (yet)

Trauma reshapes connected systems:

  • Amygdala: the threat detector becomes over-responsive.
  • Hippocampus: time-stamps/context get fuzzy, so “then” feels like “now.”
  • Prefrontal cortex: planning/soothing circuits go offline under stress.
  • HPA axis & cortisol: stress chemistry stays high → sleep/mood issues.
  • Norepinephrine: boosts memory for danger cues (hypervigilance).

Good news: these systems remain plastic. With the right tools and therapy, they can recalibrate.

4) Window of Tolerance

  • Up-tools (for numb/flat): brisk walk, cool water on wrists, upbeat music + simple movement, name 5 things you see.
  • Down-tools (for revved/panicky): long exhale (4-6), paced breathing, weighted blanket, warm drink, safe-place visual.

5) Grounding & In-the-moment tools

5-4-3-2-1

5 see · 4 touch · 3 hear · 2 smell · 1 taste. Say each out loud to anchor in the present.

Breath anchors

In for 4, out for 6 (2–3 min). Or box (4-4-4-4). Hand on chest, feel warmth + weight.

Orientation

Turn your head, name 3 safe objects, today’s date, where you are. “Right now I am safe enough.”

Safe-place visual

Recall a calm scene in detail: scents, colours, textures. Pair with the long exhale.

6) Flashbacks, Nightmares & Sleep

If a flashback hits

  • Say: “This is a memory, not the present.” Name the date and where you are.
  • Ground: feet to floor, name 5 things you can see.
  • Temperature reset: cool splash on face/wrists, or hold an ice pack briefly.
  • Call/text a safe person; keep a short script saved in your phone.

Nightmares

  • Try Imagery Rehearsal Therapy (IRT): rewrite the ending while awake; practise daily.
  • Low-light, gentle wind-down; reduce caffeine/alcohol late; consistent wake time.
  • Consider discussing prazosin (nightmares) with your GP/psychiatrist.

7) Trauma Memory — why it feels like “now”

During trauma, the brain stores intense fragments (sensations, images, sounds) with weak time-stamps. Later, cues can “replay” the fragments as if the event were current. Therapy helps re-file these memories with context so the alarm doesn’t have to fire.

  • EMDR: uses bilateral stimulation while recalling elements, aiding reconsolidation.
  • Trauma-focused CBT/CPT: updates beliefs (“I’m to blame”) with accurate narratives.
  • Narrative Exposure Therapy: builds a coherent life story integrating trauma memories.

8) Evidence-based treatments & self-care

Therapies

  • EMDR; Trauma-Focused CBT; CPT; NET
  • For cPTSD: phase-based work (stabilise → process → reconnect)

Medication

  • SSRIs/SNRIs for mood/anxiety, sleep agents short-term only
  • Discuss options/risks with your GP or psychiatrist

Daily supports

  • Sleep anchors; steady meals; light movement; low-stim “recovery hours”
  • Track wins; keep boundaries; keep alcohol low

9) For Veterans & Emergency Responders

Repeated exposure, shift work, and moral injury add unique strains. Confidential, specialist help exists:

  • Combat Stress — veteran mental health helpline & treatment
  • NHS Veterans (England, incl. Op COURAGE) / Veterans First Point (Scotland)
  • Police Care UK — police & families
  • Fire Fighters Charity — fire & rescue
  • TASC: The Ambulance Staff Charity — ambulance personnel
  • Samaritans — 24/7 listening; also workplace programmes for services

Important Note

The information on this page is for general understanding and support. It is not a substitute for professional medical, psychological, or legal advice. If you feel unable to keep yourself safe or someone else is at risk, call 999 (UK) immediately. If you’re outside the UK, contact your local emergency number.

For non-emergency concerns, consider speaking with a qualified health professional or one of the services listed above.
