Anxiety & Panic — Practical Help

Anxiety is your brain’s alarm system — useful in danger, overwhelming when it won’t switch off. This page explains what’s happening in your body, gives step-by-step tools for panic and day-to-day steadiness, and signposts UK and regional support.

1) What anxiety is (and why it feels physical)

Anxiety is a body-first alarm: the amygdala signals “threat,” the sympathetic nervous system floods you with adrenaline, breathing quickens, heart rate rises, and attention narrows. If the alarm misfires often, your system becomes sensitised — small cues trigger big reactions.

  • Common symptoms: racing heart, tight chest, nausea, trembling, dizzy, mind racing, “what-ifs”.
  • Why it sticks: we avoid what feels scary → short-term relief → the brain “learns” it was dangerous.
  • Good news: the alarm can be retrained with small, repeatable signals of safety.

Types you might recognise

  • Generalised anxiety (GAD)
  • Panic disorder
  • Social anxiety
  • Health anxiety
  • OCD (obsessions/compulsions)
  • PTSD/trauma-linked anxiety
  • Perinatal anxiety

Reassurance

You’re not “weak” — you’re activated. The work is lowering baseline activation and gently expanding what feels safe.

Micro-principles

  • Long exhale > long inhale
  • Label the feeling
  • Move a little, often
  • Approach, don’t avoid (tiny steps)

2) Panic attacks: a 4-step plan

Panic peaks fast and passes. Use this script (save it on your phone):

  1. Name it: “This is panic. It feels awful and it will pass.”
  2. Anchor breath: 4-6 breathing (in 4, out 6) for 90–120 seconds. Try humming on the exhale.
  3. Ground senses: press feet into the floor; hold a cold glass; describe 3 objects you can see.
  4. Stay put if safe: let the wave crest and fall; set a 3-minute timer and observe.

3) Fast tools (2–5 minutes)

  • Physiological sigh (double inhale, long exhale) × 5–10 cycles.
  • Butterfly tap (crossed-arm shoulder taps, left-right, slow) for 60–120 seconds.
  • Box breath 4-4-4-4 or 4-7-8 at bedtime.
  • Temperature shift: cool water on wrists/face; hold a cold can.
  • Micro-move: 60–120 seconds brisk walk, stairs, or gentle shaking of hands/arms.
  • Label & allow: “Anxiety is here; I can let it pass while I make tea.”

4) Daily steadying habits (nervous system hygiene)

Morning light

Daylight in the first hour anchors your body clock and reduces evening anxiety.

Movement minimum

10–20 minutes most days (walk, cycle, gentle yoga). Sprinkle “movement snacks.”

Breath anchor

1–2 minutes of 4-6 breathing before emails, calls, or bed.

Media boundaries

News once daily; app limits at night; batch notifications.

Connection

One honest message a day (“Heavy day — a quick check-in helps”).

Nature dose

Green view, plant care, or a short outside pause lowers baseline arousal.

5) Thought tools (accurate, not fake-positive)

Defuse the thought

“I’m having the thought that I’ll mess this up.” (creates distance)

Reframe gently

  • “I can’t cope” → “I can do the first minute.”
  • “It must be perfect” → “Done at 80% beats stuck at 0%.”

If–Then plans

If I feel the surge → Then breathe 4-6 and text a safe person.

6) Gentle exposure (retraining the alarm)

Avoidance teaches “danger.” Exposure teaches “tolerable.” Build a ladder from easy → medium → hard.

  1. List feared situations (e.g., short queue, small shop, bus 1 stop, bus 3 stops).
  2. Start easy. Breathe slowly while staying long enough for anxiety to peak and drop a little.
  3. Repeat the same rung on different days until it feels easier; then move up one rung.

Stop for the day around 5–6/10 intensity. The win is approach, not perfection.

7) Sleep, food, caffeine (basics that matter)

Sleep scaffolding

  • Consistent wake time
  • Dim screens/lights 60–90 mins before bed
  • Wind-down: warm drink + slow breath

Steady fuel

Protein + fibre each meal; don’t skip; hydrate (glass per meal). Low blood sugar mimics anxiety.

Caffeine & alcohol

Try a 2-week caffeine dial-down (half strength, then decaf after midday). Alcohol may rebound anxiety at 3–4am.

8) Work & study adjustments (small changes, big relief)

  • Clear written briefs; examples of “what good looks like”.
  • Meeting hygiene: agenda, 50-min hours, summary bullets.
  • Quiet space / camera-off allowances when possible.
  • “Landing” time between tasks; visual timers for focus blocks.
  • Flex for medical/therapy appointments.

In the UK, reasonable adjustments may be available; ask HR/student support.

9) For carers & friends (scripts that help)

  • Validate: “I believe you. This is hard and it will pass.”
  • Co-regulate: “Let’s breathe out slowly together.”
  • Offer a choice: “Tea now or a short walk?”
  • Boundary + care: “I can chat 10 minutes now and again at 7pm.”

10) Children & teens

  • Name it simply: “Your body alarm is loud. We can turn it down.”
  • Teach one tool (physiological sigh) and practise when calm first.
  • Use visual plans; reduce surprises; keep transitions gentle.
  • School link: ask for a quiet space pass and flexible deadlines.

11) When to get more help

  • Anxiety most days for weeks; avoiding normal life; sleep badly impacted.
  • Panic attacks frequent; fear of leaving home; using alcohol to cope.
  • Thoughts of self-harm or you feel unsafe — treat as urgent.

Talk to your GP about NHS Talking Therapies (England) / NHS Psychological Therapies (Scotland/Wales/NI). Private options exist; check therapist registers and ask about CBT/ACT/trauma-informed care.

Important Note

The information on this page is for general understanding and support. It is not a substitute for professional medical, psychological, or legal advice. If you feel unable to keep yourself safe or someone else is at risk, call 999 (UK) immediately. If you’re outside the UK, contact your local emergency number.

For non-emergency concerns, consider speaking with a qualified health professional or an appropriate support service listed above.
