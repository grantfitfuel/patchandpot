Carer Support

Caring for someone you love is demanding, meaningful work. This page offers practical calm tools, day-to-day structure, dementia-friendly communication ideas, safety checklists, money/rights pointers, and crisis planning. Take what helps; leave the rest.

2-Minute Reset (box breathing)

Use when you feel overwhelmed, angry, or shaky.

  1. Exhale fully.
  2. Inhale through the nose – 4 counts.
  3. Hold – 4 counts.
  4. Exhale through the mouth – 4 counts.
  5. Hold empty – 4 counts. Repeat 4–6 cycles.

Grounding in 30s (5–4–3–2–1)

Helps your body exit “fight/flight”.

Notice: 5 things you see, 4 feel, 3 hear, 2 smell, 1 taste. Name them slowly.

Micro-recovery list

Keep it on your phone or fridge.

  • 3 slow breaths by an open window
  • Drink a full glass of water
  • Step outside for one minute of daylight
  • Neck/shoulder circles (10 slow each way)
  • Message a friend: “Thinking of you”

Daily Flow That Protects You

Tiny, repeatable anchors beat big plans.

  • Morning: water + medication check + 2-minute calm
  • Midday: both eat something simple; 5-minute movement
  • Evening: write tomorrow’s 3 priorities only

Why it works: your nervous system trusts repeated cues more than promises.

Boundaries (kind and firm)

  • “I can help for 10 minutes, then I need to phone the GP.”
  • “Let’s try this first, then we’ll decide.”
  • “I’m going to make tea. We’ll talk after.”

Respite You Can Actually Use

  • Neighbour swap: 30 minutes while you walk
  • Shower window: safe seat + favourite audio
  • Door-frame stretches while the kettle boils

Dementia-Friendly Communication

  • One instruction at a time, in the order it’s done
  • Use yes/no or A/B choices; avoid open questions
  • Approach from the front, eye level; gentle touch on forearm
  • Blame the task, not the person: “This door is fiddly”
  • Validate feelings: “That sounds frustrating. Let’s do it together.”

Safety First Checklist

Review monthly (set a phone reminder).

  • Medication organiser up to date + alarms
  • ICE card by phone with NHS number & allergies
  • Trip hazards cleared; night-light fitted
  • Kitchen: kettle, hob, knives stored safely
  • Front-door plan: who answers, what is said

When Behaviour Spikes

Think need → trigger → environment.

  • Need: hungry, cold, pain, toilet, bored?
  • Trigger: noise, clutter, rushed instructions?
  • Environment: reduce stimuli; use music; offer a simple task (fold towels, water plants)

Money & Benefits (UK)

  • Carer’s Allowance (if caring ≥35 hrs/wk and under earnings limit)
  • Attendance Allowance / PIP for the person you care for
  • Council tax reduction (severe mental impairment rules)

Policies change; check with local advice services.

Your Rights as a Carer

Ask your local authority for a Carer’s Assessment.

  • Support with respite or short breaks
  • Training (safe moving & handling)
  • Equipment adaptations and home safety

Medical Navigation

  • Request double appointments; bring a written update
  • Use GP consent forms so staff can speak with you
  • Keep a one-page “About Me/Us”: conditions, meds, baseline, likes, concerns

If You’re at Breaking Point

Please reach out for real-time help.

  • If anyone is in immediate danger, call your local emergency number
  • Contact your GP or out-of-hours for urgent mental health support
  • Search “crisis helpline” + your area for listening lines

Online pages help, but emergencies need human support.

Plan for Tough Days

  • Who can sit with your loved one for 30–60 mins?
  • What calms you fastest? (walk, shower, breathing, music)
  • One message to send a friend: “Today’s hard — can you check in?”

Gentle Reframes

  • “I should cope better” → “I’m doing a hard thing with the energy I have.”
  • “I’m failing” → “I’m learning. Small adjustments count.”
  • “No one gets it” → “Some do. I’ll tell one person what today’s like.”

Helpful Links

  • Carers UK (info, money checks, forum)
  • Alzheimer’s Society (dementia support)
  • Mind (mental health information)

Links are general; use local services for area-specific support.
