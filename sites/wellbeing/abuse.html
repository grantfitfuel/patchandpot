Abuse Support

Abuse can be physical, emotional, sexual, financial, or controlling/monitoring behaviour (including tech-based). This page helps you spot warning signs, plan for safety, protect your devices, and reach real-world help. If anyone is in immediate danger, contact emergency services in your area.

Common Red Flags

  • Isolation: discouraging or blocking contact with friends/family.
  • Control: monitoring your phone, location, or spending.
  • Insults & belittling: repeated criticism, name-calling, humiliation.
  • Financial control: taking your money, debts in your name, tight “allowances”.
  • Threats: to you, children, pets, immigration status, outing private info.
  • Physical/sexual harm: any unwanted touch, coerced intimacy, injuries.

Types of Abuse

Abuse doesn’t have to leave marks.

  • Emotional / psychological
  • Coercive control
  • Physical / sexual
  • Financial / economic
  • Digital / tech-enabled (spyware, stalking, doxxing)

The Cycle (Why It’s Hard to Leave)

Understanding patterns can reduce self-blame.

Tension builds → incident → minimising or apologies → brief calm → repeat. Frequency and severity often increase over time.

Digital Safety (Quick Wins)

  • Use a device they don’t access (library, friend) for sensitive searches.
  • Change passwords and enable 2FA for email, banking, socials.
  • Check app permissions; remove unknown “device admin” or tracking apps.
  • Turn off location sharing; review “Find My” / Google Location History.
  • Hidden folder: photo evidence, serial numbers, important documents.

If You Suspect Tracking

  • Scan for unknown AirTags/tiles; use built-in scanners where available.
  • Car checks: wheel wells, glove box, boot lining for tags.
  • Consider a “clean” phone for planning; keep usual patterns until safe.

Prioritise safety over confronting the behaviour.

Evidence & Logging

  • Keep a dated log (what happened, when, witnesses).
  • Photograph injuries/damage next to a clock/calendar.
  • Save messages/voicemails; back up to cloud or trusted contact.

Safety Plan (At Home)

  • Agree a code word with a trusted person that means “call me now / call for help”.
  • Identify safest room (door between you and exits, fewer hazards).
  • Pack a small grab-bag (copies of ID, meds, cash card, spare keys, charger).
  • Teach children a simple safety step (go to neighbour, call a relative).

Exit Checklist (If Leaving)

  • Documents: ID, passports, birth certs, benefits, tenancy/mortgage.
  • Finances: open a separate account; change online banking passwords.
  • Transport plan: route, time, backup plan, who meets you.
  • After leaving: change locks if possible; update schools/work discreetly.

Legal & Housing

  • Ask about protective/restraining orders and how to apply.
  • Speak to housing about emergency options and locks/security.
  • Keep copies of reports and your evidence log.

After Leaving

Recovery is gradual; safety first.

  • Rebuild anchors: food, water, sleep window, daylight.
  • Limit contact; use written channels; keep records.
  • Support: local services, counselling, peer groups.

Children

  • Explain changes with clear, simple language.
  • Maintain routines where possible.
  • Share school handover plans and key contacts.

Steady Yourself

  • Grounding and breathing tools (2 minutes).
  • One supportive conversation a week.
  • Outdoor time or plant care for predictable calm.
